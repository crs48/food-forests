Permaculture is often described as "permanent agriculture," but its founders —
the Australians **Bill Mollison** and **David Holmgren**, working in the 1970s —
meant something broader: a *permanent culture*, a way of designing human systems
that could last. A food forest is one beautiful expression of it, but the same
thinking applies to water, buildings, energy, and community.

At its heart sit three simple ethics.

## The three ethics

- **Earth Care** — provision for all living systems to continue and thrive.
- **People Care** — provision for people to access what they need for a decent
  life.
- **Fair Share** — set limits to consumption and return the surplus back into
  caring for the earth and people.

Everything else is in service of these three.

## The twelve design principles

Holmgren later distilled the practice into twelve principles, each paired with a
folk proverb. They are less a checklist than a set of lenses — ways to look at a
problem until a good design reveals itself.

A few that matter most when you are just starting out:

1. **Observe and interact.** Watch your land across the seasons before you
   plant. Where does frost settle? Where does water run? This is the single most
   valuable thing a beginner can do.
2. **Catch and store energy.** Harvest abundance when it comes — rainwater in
   winter, biomass in autumn, sun in summer — and save it for the lean times.
3. **Obtain a yield.** A design has to actually feed the people tending it, or
   they will stop.
4. **Use small and slow solutions.** Small systems are easier to build, cheaper
   to fix, and gentler to learn from. Start tiny.
5. **Use and value diversity.** Don't put all your eggs in one basket — a
   diverse system is a resilient one.
6. **Integrate rather than segregate.** Place things so they help each other; the
   right relationships do the work for you.

## Why the ethics come first

It is tempting to jump straight to swales and guilds and plant lists. But the
ethics are what keep permaculture from becoming a bag of clever tricks. They ask,
again and again: *does this care for the earth, care for people, and share
fairly?* If the answer is yes, you are probably on the right track.
