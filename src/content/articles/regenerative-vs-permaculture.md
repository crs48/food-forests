You will hear *food forest*, *permaculture*, and *regenerative agriculture* used
almost interchangeably. They share deep roots — a respect for soil life, a
distrust of bare ground and synthetic shortcuts, a love of diversity — but they
are not the same thing, and the differences are worth knowing.

## A quick way to tell them apart

- **Permaculture** is a **design methodology**. It asks: *how do I design this
  whole site — water, plants, buildings, people — so it thrives?* It tends to
  work at the scale of a garden or smallholding and leans heavily on perennials.
- **A food forest** is a **specific technique**: one particular, perennial,
  layered planting that a permaculture design might include.
- **Regenerative agriculture** is a set of **practices for working farms**. It
  asks a narrower, powerful question: *how do I rebuild this soil?* — and it is
  often applied to broadacre row crops and grazing land, not just gardens.

Put simply: permaculture *designs the whole*, a food forest is *one part of the
whole*, and regenerative agriculture *heals the ground beneath it*.

## What "regenerative" adds

The word was coined at the Rodale Institute in the 1980s to describe farming that
goes beyond *sustainable* — beyond merely maintaining the land — to actively
*improve* it over time. Its core practices are refreshingly concrete:

- **Keep the soil covered** with cover crops and living roots year-round.
- **Disturb it as little as possible** — no-till or reduced tillage protects soil
  structure, fungi, and stored carbon.
- **Grow diversity**, in rotations and polycultures, to break pest cycles.
- **Bring animals back**, grazing them in a way that mimics wild herds so pasture
  recovers and roots run deep.

The overarching aim is to rebuild soil carbon and biology — with real benefits for
water, resilience, and, potentially, the climate.

## A note on the claims

Regenerative agriculture is genuinely exciting, and it has also attracted big
promises. Some — like the more sweeping claims about reversing desertification
through grazing alone — remain **scientifically debated**. That is not a reason
for cynicism; it is a reason to stay curious, look at real results, and be honest
about what we do and don't yet know.

## Why keep them straight?

Because the words point you toward different tools. If you have a backyard and
want an edible ecosystem, you want *food forest* and *permaculture* thinking. If
you steward pasture or a grain field, *regenerative* practices will speak more
directly to your land. Most of us, happily, can borrow from all three.
