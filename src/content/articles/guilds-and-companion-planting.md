If the seven layers describe the *structure* of a food forest, **guilds**
describe its *relationships*. A guild is a small community of plants gathered
around a central element — usually a fruit tree — chosen so that each member
does a job the others benefit from. Done well, a guild is largely
self-supporting: it feeds itself, protects itself, and needs less from you.

## The classic parts of a guild

Think of a young apple tree at the centre. Around it you might plant:

- **A nitrogen fixer** — clover, lupin, or a shrub like sea buckthorn — that
  pulls nitrogen from the air and shares it, feeding the tree for free.
- **A dynamic accumulator** — comfrey is the favourite — whose deep roots mine
  minerals from far below, then hand them back when you chop its leaves for
  mulch.
- **Pollinator plants** — flowering herbs that draw in bees and hoverflies to set
  fruit and eat pests.
- **Pest-confusers** — alliums like garlic and chives, whose scent muddles the
  insects looking for your tree.
- **Ground cover** — strawberries or creeping herbs that shade the soil, hold
  moisture, and crowd out weeds.

None of these are doing you a favour out of kindness — each simply thrives while,
as a side effect, making its neighbours' lives better. That is the quiet genius
of a guild.

## The oldest guild of all

The idea is ancient. The Indigenous **Three Sisters** — corn, beans, and squash —
is a guild you can plant in a single season: corn gives the beans a trellis, the
beans fix nitrogen for the corn, and broad squash leaves shade the soil as living
mulch. Together they even make a more complete meal than any one of them alone.

## Start with one

You don't need to design a whole forest to begin. Plant *one* tree and its guild,
watch how the pieces support each other for a season or two, and you will have
learned more than any book can teach. From there, the forest grows one guild at a
time. Here's [how to start small](/start).
