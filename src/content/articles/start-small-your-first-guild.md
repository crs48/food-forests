The most common way a food forest dies is being too ambitious on day one. The
antidote is a core permaculture principle: **use small and slow solutions**.
Start with a single guild — one tree and its supporting cast — and let it teach
you before you scale up.

## Before you plant anything: watch

Spend a season *observing* your space. Notice where the sun falls in the morning
and afternoon, where water pools after rain, where frost lingers, and what
already grows well. This "protracted observation" is free, and it will save you
from planting a sun-lover in the shade.

## A first guild you can actually plant

Pick a small fruit tree suited to your climate and give it a few companions:

- the **tree** at the centre (an apple on dwarfing rootstock is forgiving);
- a ring of **comfrey** and **daffodils** at the drip line (comfrey mulches and
  feeds; the bulbs deter grass and rodents);
- **clover** or another nitrogen fixer as ground cover;
- a few **flowering herbs** — chives, dill, yarrow — for pollinators and pest
  control;
- **strawberries** to knit across the soil.

That's it. A tree and a handful of friends. It will fit in a corner of a modest
yard.

## Feed the soil, then be patient

Mulch generously, water while things establish, and then — this is the hard part
— wait. A guild takes a couple of years to find its rhythm, and a food forest
three years or more to become largely self-sustaining. The work drops as the
system matures.

## Then, one guild at a time

Once your first guild is thriving and you understand *why*, add another. And
another. That is how every food forest on this site began — not with a
masterplan, but with one tree and the patience to watch it grow. When you're
ready for the bigger picture, read [what a food forest
is](/learn/what-is-a-food-forest) and explore [the seven layers](/layers).
