A food forest — also called a *forest garden* — is a garden designed to work
like a young woodland. Instead of neat rows of vegetables that you replant every
spring, it layers fruit and nut trees, berry bushes, herbs, ground covers, roots,
and climbing vines into a single, self-supporting community that produces food,
medicine, and materials year after year.

The idea is old. Tropical home gardens across India, Southeast Asia, Central
America, and Africa have layered edible plants around the home for millennia, and
Indigenous peoples worldwide tended polycultures — like the North American
*Three Sisters* of corn, beans, and squash — long before anyone wrote it down. In
the 1980s the British horticulturist **Robert Hart** adapted that structure to a
temperate climate and gave us the language of forest gardening still used today.

## What makes it different

A conventional vegetable garden fights nature a little: bare soil, annual plants,
constant tilling, feeding, and weeding. A food forest tries to *cooperate* with
nature instead.

- **Perennial, not annual.** Most plants live for years, so you plant once and
  harvest for a long time.
- **Layered, not flat.** Plants of different heights share the same ground, so a
  small plot grows a surprising amount.
- **A polyculture, not a monoculture.** Diversity confuses pests, spreads risk,
  and builds resilience.
- **Low-maintenance once established.** There is real effort up front, but a
  mature system largely feeds and mulches itself.

## The catch (and the gift)

A food forest is slow. It can take three years or more to settle in, and longer
to mature. But that patience is repaid: as the trees grow and the soil comes
alive, the work goes *down* while the harvest goes *up*. You are not building a
garden so much as starting an ecosystem — and then letting it do most of the
growing.
