One of permaculture's favourite ideas is **stacking functions**: designing so
that each element does several jobs at once. A chicken isn't just eggs — it's also
pest control, manure, and tillage. The same is true of plants. The most valuable
species in a food forest are the ones working the hardest behind the scenes.

## Nitrogen fixers — free fertiliser from thin air

Nitrogen is the nutrient plants crave most, and certain species partner with soil
bacteria to pull it straight out of the air and into the ground. Plant these among
your fruit trees and you cut your need for fertiliser dramatically:

- **Ground layer:** clover, vetch, lupin, peas and beans.
- **Shrub layer:** sea buckthorn, autumn olive, Siberian pea shrub.
- **Canopy layer:** alder, black locust.

Many growers plant *extra* nitrogen fixers, then chop them down over time as the
fruit trees mature — a technique sometimes called "chop and drop."

## Dynamic accumulators — miners of the deep

Some plants send roots far below the reach of others, drawing up minerals from the
subsoil and storing them in their leaves. Cut those leaves and lay them on the
ground, and you've transferred that fertility to the surface where shallow-rooted
plants can use it. **Comfrey** is the classic example; dandelion, yarrow, and
nettle do similar work.

## Multi-taskers worth knowing

- **Comfrey** — dynamic accumulator, mulch factory, pollinator plant, all in one.
- **Daffodils** — a living fence against grass and burrowing rodents around a
  tree's base.
- **Yarrow** — pollinator magnet, medicinal herb, and accumulator.
- **Clover** — nitrogen fixer, ground cover, and bee forage together.

## The takeaway

When you choose a plant for a food forest, ask what *else* it can do. Fruit is
lovely, but the plants that feed the soil, shelter the pollinators, and mulch
their neighbours are what make the whole system stand on its own. Design a guild
around them and you've read the whole story: see [guilds & companion
planting](/learn/guilds-and-companion-planting).
